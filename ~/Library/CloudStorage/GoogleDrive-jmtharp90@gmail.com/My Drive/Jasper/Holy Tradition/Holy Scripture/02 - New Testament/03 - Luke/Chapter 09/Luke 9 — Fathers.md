> WHOEVER RECEIVES THIS LITTLE CHILD IN MY NAME 
> RECEIVES ME

> He ...also set the child in their midst, by the very sight abashing them, 
> and persuading them to be in like manner lowly and artless, since the little 
> child is pure from envy and from vainglory, and from longing for the first 
> place ...Not only courage is wanted then, nor wisdom, but these virtues also: 
> humility I mean, and simplicity ...The little child, whether it be insulted and 
> beaten, or honored and glorified, neither is it moved by the one to im- 
> patience or envy, nor lifted up by the other ...If you yourselves become like 
> this, not only will you receive a great reward, but also if for my sake you 
> honor others who are such, even for your honor to them I appoint you a 
> kingdom as your recompense. Or rather, He sets down what is far greater, 
> saying, ‘he receives Me.’ For by a little child here, He means the men who 
> are in the same way simple and lowly and abject and contemptible in the 
> judgment of the common sort.
>
> St. John Chrysostom. Homily LVIII on Matthew XVIII, 3.

> [!cite] John Chrysostom — Homily XXVII on Matthew 8, 6,7
> LET THE DEAD BURY THEIR OWN DEAD, BUT YOU GO AND 
> PREACH THE KINGDOM OF GOD
>
> Did you notice the difference? Did you see how one impudently says, 
> ‘I will follow You wherever You go,’ but this other, although asking a thing 
> of sacred duty, says, ‘Allow me.’ Yet He did not allow him, but says, Let the 
> dead bury their dead, but do you follow Me. For in every case He had 
> regard to the intention. And why did He not allow him? one may ask. Be- 
> cause, on the one hand, there were those who would fulfill that duty, and 
> the dead was not going to remain unburied; on the other, it was not fit for 
> this man to be taken away from weightier matters ...And because He who 
> was dead, was, at least as I suppose, of the unbelievers ...
>
> OF SPIRITUAL PRIORITIES
>
> Was it not then, one may say, extreme ingratitude, not to be present at 
> the burial of his father? If indeed he did so out of negligence, it was in- 
> gratitude, but if in order not to interrupt a more needful work, his departing 
> would most surely not have been of extreme inconsideration. For Jesus for- 
> bad him, not as commanding to think lightly of the honor due to our 
> parents, but signifying that nothing ought to be to us more urgent than the 
> things of heaven, and that we ought with all diligence to cleave to these, and 
> not to put them off for ever so little, though our engagements might be ex- 
> ceedingly indispensable and pressing. For what can be more needful than 
> to bury a father? ...But if one ought not to spend even as much time as is re- 
> quired for a father’s burial, neither is it safe to be parted even so long from 
> our spiritual concerns. Consider what we deserve, who all our time stand 
> off from the things that pertain to Christ, and prefer things very ordinary to 
> such as are needful, and are remiss, when there is nothing to press on us?...
>
> We should admire the instructiveness of His teaching, that He nailed 
> him fast to His word, and with this freed him ...For after the burial he must 
> of necessity proceed to inquire about the will, then about the distribution of 
> the inheritance, and all the other things that follow thereupon; and thus 
> waves after waves coming in succession upon him, would bear him away

> prophets; while others, having received sal-

> He became flesh according to the Scripture. The blessed Peter, therefore, 
> professed faith in Him, lending ...his words to the whole company of the 
> holy apostles, and acting as spokesman for them all, as being more accurate 
> than the rest.

> that the blessed disciple said, “You are the Christ, the Son of the living 
> God’; but the wise Luke, summing up so to speak the purport, agrees with
