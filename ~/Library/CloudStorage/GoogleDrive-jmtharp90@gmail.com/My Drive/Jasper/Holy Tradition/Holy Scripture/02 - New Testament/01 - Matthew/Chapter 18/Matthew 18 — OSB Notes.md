### [[Matthew 18#v1|18:1-4]]
^v1
> [!note] 18:1-4 ^f4258
> This question indicates a selfish interest in worldly power. Pointing to a **little child** as the model of true discipleship, Jesus emphasizes the virtues required for entrance into the **kingdom of heaven**: humility, dependence, lowliness, simplicity, obedience, and a willingness to love and be loved. In Orthodox iconography, St. Ignatius of Antioch is depicted as this child. In certain legends of saints, he is the boy who gave the loaves and fishes ([[John 6#v9|Jn 6:9]]).

### [[Matthew 18#v6|18:6]]

> [!note] 18:6 ^f4259

### [[Matthew 18#v8|18:8]]
^v8
> [!note] 18:8 ^f4260
> See also [[Matthew 5#v29|5:29]]. The reference to mutilation is an illustration of decisive action to avoid sin and does not advocate literal amputation. This also refers to harmful relationships that must be severed for the salvation of all parties (see [[Luke 14#v26|Lk 14:26]]; [[I Corinthians 5#v5|1Co 5:5]]).

However, the angels of humble people have greater boldness and greater honor before **the face **of God because of the humility of the person they guard. It is not the nature of God, but the weakness of men, that requires the angels' service.

### [[Matthew 18#v12|18:12]]
^v12
> [!note] 18:12 ^f4261
> Unlike earthly shepherds, Christ sees such value in **one **sheep that He will leave the others at risk to save it. The **ninety-nine **sheep represent the righteous who remain faithful to God ([[Luke 15#v7|Lk 15:7]]). According to certain Fathers, this is also an image of the Incarnation in which the ninety-nine represent the angels in heaven; Christ descended from heaven to pursue the one sheep—man—who had fallen into corruption on earth.

### [[Matthew 18#v15|18:15-20]]
^v15
> [!note] 18:15-20 ^f4262
> Church discipline is based on mutual correction in three expanding stages. Sin and correction are to remain private unless the offender refuses to repent. All correction must be done with great care and humility, with the highest concern being the salvation of the offender (see [[I Corinthians 5#v5|1Co 5:5]]; [[Galatians 6#v1|Gal 6:1]]). Nevertheless, correction must take place so that the sin does not spread to others as well.

^v18
> [!note] 18:18-20 ^f4264
> The authority to **bind **and **loose **sins is given to the apostles and transmitted to the bishops and presbyters they ordained. The sinner, “seeing that he is not only cast out of the Church, but that the bond of his sin will remain in Heaven, he may turn and become gentle” (JohnChr).

### [[Matthew 18#v21|18:21-35]]
^v21
> [!note] 18:21-35 ^f4265
> **Seventy times seven**: Symbolic of an unlimited amount. This parable illustrates the need for unlimited forgiveness. **Ten thousand talents **(v. [[Matthew 18#v24|24]]) is an impossible sum, more than a laborer could earn in several lifetimes. **A hundred denarii **(v. [[Matthew 18#v28|28]]) is a significant amount from an earthly perspective (about three months' wages), yet it is a pittance when compared to the debt owed the **king**. God not only stays the punishment we deserve, but forgives us the entire debt as well (v. [[Matthew 18#v27|27]]). Because God forgives us, we in turn are required to grant the gift of forgiveness to others.

### [[Matthew 18#v25|18:25]]
^v25
> [!note] 18:25 ^f4266
> Certain Fathers give a spiritual interpretation to the punishment described here. The man represents the soul, the wife represents the body, and the children represent a person's deeds. Thus, the body and the deeds are given over to slavery, that is to Satan, so that the soul might possibly be saved (see [[I Corinthians 5#v5|1Co 5:5]]).
