### [[Ezekiel 1#v1|1:1]]
^v1
> [!note] 1:1 ^f3882
> St. Gregory the Great comments that after establishing the time and place in history, the Prophet Ezekiel mystically “shows forth the Lord,” for in Christ's **thirtieth year** He was baptized and the **heavens were opened** (see [[Matthew 3#v17|Mt 3:17]]).

### [[Ezekiel 1#v3|1:3-4]]
^v3
> [!note] 1:3-4 ^f3883
> St. Gregory writes that **Ezekiel** recognized the Incarnation of the Only Begotten when he saw the **hand of the Lord.** The **amber** signifies Christ, who “tempered” His divinity by taking on humanity, that humanity by grace might take on divinity. “Like amber in the fire, God is made man in persecution.”

### [[Ezekiel 1#v5|1:5-9]]
^v5
> [!note] 1:5-9 ^f3884
> Ezekiel identifies the **four living creatures** as cherubim ([[Ezekiel 10#v9|10:9–16]]), the guardians of the throne of God ([[Exodus 25#v10|Ex 25:10–22]]; [[III Kingdoms 6#v23|3Kg 6:23–28]]). Like those of St. John's vision (see [[Revelation 4#v6|Rev 4:6–9]]), they are seen by the Fathers to typify the Four Evangelists, while their unity in the spirit (vv. [[Ezekiel 1#v12|12]], [[Ezekiel 1#v20|20]]) illustrates the spread of the gospel.

### [[Ezekiel 1#v10|1:10]]
^v10
> [!note] 1:10 ^f3885
> St. Gregory the Great comments that the **man **is Matthew, because he begins his Gospel with Christ's genealogy ([[Matthew 1#v1|Mt 1:1]]). The **lion** is Mark, “because of the crying in the wilderness” ([[Mark 1#v1|Mk 1:1]]). The** ox** is Luke, “because he started with a sacrifice” ([[Luke 1#v8|Lk 1:8]]). The** eagle** is John, who “stretched toward the very substance of divinity” ([[John 1#v1|Jn 1:1]]). Gregory also sees in them types of Christ, who became a man in the Incarnation, an ox on the Cross, a lion in the Resurrection, and an eagle in the Ascension. Finally, says Gregory, they symbolize “every one of the elect, who are perfect in the way of God,” whose virtues are reason, self-sacrifice, fortitude, and contemplation of heavenly things.

### [[Ezekiel 1#v13|1:13]]
^v13
> [!note] 1:13 ^f3886
> The **burning coals** (v. [[Ezekiel 1#v13|13]]), writes St. Gregory, are “holy men” (see [[Isaiah 6#v6|Is 6:6]] and note). The **lamps **signify the light of the gospel (see [[Judges 7#v16|Jdg 7:16]]).

### [[Ezekiel 1#v14|1:14-21]]
^v14
> [!note] 1:14-21 ^f3887
> The **wheels** signify Holy Scripture, and the **wheel within a wheel **illustrates that “the New Testament lay hidden by allegory in the letter of the Old Testament” (GrgGt).

### [[Ezekiel 1#v22|1:22]]
^v22
> [!note] 1:22 ^f3888
> St. Gregory sees in the **wings** an image of the virtues of the righteous.

### [[Ezekiel 1#v25|1:25]]
^v25
> [!note] 1:25 ^f3889
> The **man** on the **sapphire** . . . **throne** prefigures the Incarnation of the Son of God from the Virgin Mary.

### [[Ezekiel 1#v27|1:27]]
^v27
> [!note] 1:27 ^f3890
> The **rainbow** images the new covenant (see [[Genesis 9#v13|Gn 9:13–15]]).
