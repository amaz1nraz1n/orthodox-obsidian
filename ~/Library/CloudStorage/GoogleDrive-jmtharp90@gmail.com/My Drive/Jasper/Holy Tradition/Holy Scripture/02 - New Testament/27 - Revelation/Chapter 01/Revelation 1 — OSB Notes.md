### [[Revelation 1#v1|1:1]]
^v1
> [!note] 1:1 ^f6053
> **Revelation** or Apocalypse (Gr. *apokalypsis*) means literally to unconceal, unveil, or reveal. It was used anciently of a coded message that must get through without falling into the wrong hands. God Himself is the ultimate source of all revelation (see [[Daniel 2#v28|Dan 2:28]], [[Daniel 2#v29|29]], [[Daniel 2#v45|45]]), but it is the Son, Jesus Christ—who revealed Himself to the prophets of the OT before He became flesh and now is made flesh—who mediates this unveiling (see [[John 1#v18|Jn 1:18]]; [[John 4#v19|4:19–23]]; [[John 7#v16|7:16]]; [[John 8#v26|8:26]], [[John 8#v40|40]]; [[John 12#v49|12:49]]; [[John 15#v15|15:15]]; [[John 17#v8|17:8]]). This revelation concerns not only the age to come, but certain things from the past and from this present age as well. **Things which must** tells us this is not a haphazard presentation. **Shortly take place** indicates God's time, which is always imminent. It may take a lot of earth's time.

### [[Revelation 1#v2|1:2]]
^v2
> [!note] 1:2 ^f6054
> **The word of God** refers to the Son of God. **Testimony of Jesus** means a witness concerning Jesus. It is John testifying of Christ (see 4:21), not Jesus testifying of Himself. **Bore witness** (from Gr. *martyreo*, a verb) and *testimony* (from Gr. *martyria*, a noun) are forms of the word from which we obtain “martyr.” Persecution is the consequence of witnessing. The martyrs (see [[Hebrews 12#v1|Heb 12:1]]) are those who testify or bear witness, sealing it with their blood in death.

### [[Revelation 1#v3|1:3]]
^v3
> [!note] 1:3 ^f6055
> A blessing is promised to those who read and hear (see [[Luke 11#v28|Lk 11:28]]). This is the first of the seven beatitudes of the Revelation, found also in [[Revelation 14#v13|14:13]]; [[Revelation 16#v15|16:15]]; [[Revelation 19#v9|19:9]]; [[Revelation 20#v6|20:6]]; [[Revelation 22#v7|22:7]], [[Revelation 22#v14|14]]. **Blessed** here are both the reader and the hearers (the assembled faithful), provided they heed what is read—for faith and works are inseparable. Time (Gr. *kairos*) means here an eschatological decisive moment—a time of judgment, a critical moment, the time when all is fulfilled.

### [[Revelation 1#v4|1:4]]
^v4
> [!note] 1:4 ^f6056
> Church tradition maintains St. John dwelt and was bishop in Ephesus, in an area where **the seven churches** were located along a major roadway. The number *seven* signifies fullness, suggesting the entire Church is also in view.
>
> The doxology is Trinitarian, involving the Father (vv. [[Revelation 1#v4|4]], [[Revelation 1#v6|6]]), the Spirit (v. [[Revelation 1#v4|4]]), and the Son (vv. [[Revelation 1#v5|5]], [[Revelation 1#v6|6]]). This initial greeting (lit., “the Existing, the Was, and the Coming”) may express the Father, the one **who is** ([[Exodus 3#v14|Ex 3:14]]); the Son, **who was** ([[John 1#v1|Jn 1:1]]); and the Holy Spirit, **who is to come** ([[Acts 2#v1|Acts 2]]) at Pentecost and shall always be present. Or it may denote the character of the Holy One, who is eternally present and exercises lordship throughout history (see [[Hebrews 13#v8|Heb 13:8]]). God reveals the meaning of the present in light of the past and the age to come. This title may be a paraphrase of the Tetragrammaton, YHWH (“I Am”), of [[Exodus 3#v14|Ex 3:14]].
>
> **Seven** is the number of fullness or completion. The *seven* **Spirits** of God most likely refers to the Holy Spirit and His several gifts, as this phrase is included in the blessing with the Father and the Son. Alternately the term could refer to the seven archangels who, according to Jewish tradition, stand before the throne of God ([[Tobit 12#v15|Tb 12:15]]; see also 1En 20:1–8; 90:21, 22; TLev 8:2).

### [[Revelation 1#v5|1:5]]
^v5
> [!note] 1:5 ^f6057
> Jesus Christ is presented as the Risen Savior, Lord of all (see [[Zechariah 12#v10|Zec 12:10]]), giving hope to the early Christians that the Church will not always be dominated by a cruel state. Instead of **washed**, many Greek texts read “freed.” The term **witness** (Gr. *martys*), used only here and in [[Revelation 3#v14|3:14]] in the entire NT, refers to Christ, the authentic *witness* of all divine revelation; all that God has revealed is summed up in His life, witness, Passion, Resurrection, and exaltation. He has inaugurated the new age, for He is **firstborn from the dead** in His humanity and has achieved a universal sovereignty by His death, Resurrection, and revelation of His Kingdom for the world's salvation.

> [!info] 1:5a ^fvar776
> NU-Text reads *loves us and freed; *M-Text reads *loves us and washed.*

### [[Revelation 1#v6|1:6]]
^v6
> [!note] 1:6 ^f6058
> Those joined to the body of Christ in baptism comprise the messianic royal priesthood promised of old (see [[Exodus 19#v5|Ex 19:5]], [[Exodus 19#v6|6]]; [[Isaiah 61#v6|Is 61:6]]; [[I Peter 2#v9|1Pt 2:9]]; and the Anaphora of the Liturgy of St. Basil). This priestly ministry is to offer the world back to God in a sacrifice of praise and thanksgiving—eucharistically—as in the Orthodox Church's Divine Liturgy. The universe itself thus becomes hallowed, transfigured, and sacramental. **Amen** is Semitic. It signifies ratification: an acknowledgment of something trustworthy.

> [!info] 1:6a ^fvar777
> NU-Text and M-Text read *a kingdom.*

### [[Revelation 1#v8|1:8]]
^v8
> [!note] 1:8 ^f6059
> **Alpha **and** Omega**, the first and last letters of the Greek alphabet, along with **Beginning **and** End** signify the eternality of God, who is the origin and destiny of all things. Its attribution to the Son proves His divinity. History—in fact, all of creation—begins and is completed in the Creator ([[Isaiah 44#v6|Is 44:6]]). **Almighty** (Gr. *Pantocrator*) is used throughout the OT and is found ten times in the NT—nine of which are in Revelation. The inscription that adorns the central dome of a properly appointed Orthodox temple bears the words of this verse; the fresco depicts the Lord Jesus, since He is the One who shows us the Father, and since He has used the title “Alpha and Omega” for Himself ([[Revelation 22#v13|22:13]]).

> [!info] 1:8a ^fvar778
> NU-Text and M-Text omit *the Beginning and the End. *

> [!info] 1:8b ^fvar779
> NU-Text and M-Text add *God.*

### [[Revelation 1#v9|1:9]]
^v9
> [!info] 1:9a ^fvar780
> NU-Text and M-Text omit *both.*

> [!bg] 1:9b ^fback1
> **Patmos**: A small rocky island 40 miles off the western coast of modern Turkey, fifty miles south of Ephesus, to which the Romans exiled criminals. John's preaching must have been considered a seditious threat to the public interest if he was indeed a prisoner there.

### [[Revelation 1#v10|1:10]]
^v10
> [!note] 1:10 ^f6060
> **In the Spirit** may mean John received the revelation in a visionary ecstasy (see [[Ezekiel 3#v12|Ezk 3:12]]), but more probably that he was in the worship (“in Spirit and in truth”) of the Lord. **The Lord's Day** is the earliest reference to the Christian name for Sunday. The Didache and St. Ignatius of Antioch show this name was used very early for the day when Christians gathered to celebrate the Resurrection in the Holy Eucharist. As a fulfillment of the first day of the week of the old creation, Sunday becomes the “eighth day,” the “first day of the new creation.” The term “eighth day” is seen in 2En 33:1 and inaugurates the first day of the timeless age to come. The **loud voice, as of a trumpet** is a traditional, eschatological, apocalyptic introduction describing an appearance of the Lord (see [[Exodus 19#v16|Ex 19:16]], [[Exodus 19#v19|19]]; [[Matthew 24#v31|Mt 24:31]]; [[I Corinthians 15#v52|1Co 15:52]]; [[I Thessalonians 4#v16|1Th 4:16]]).

### [[Revelation 1#v11|1:11-12]]
^v11
> [!note] 1:11-12 ^f6061
> The glorified Christ introduces himself as **the** **Alpha and the Omega, the First and the Last**, thus identifying Himself with God the Father (v. [[Revelation 1#v8|8]]; [[Revelation 22#v13|22:13]]). His position amidst the seven lampstands signifies His presence in the Church (see [[Matthew 5#v14|Mt 5:14]]). The **seven golden lampstands**, a fusion of the great menorah of the temple with its seven lamps (see [[Exodus 25#v31|Ex 25:31–37]]; [[Zechariah 4#v2|Zec 4:2]]; [[Hebrews 9#v2|Heb 9:2]]) and the golden lampstands of Solomon's temple ([[III Kingdoms 7#v35|3Kg 7:35]]), represent the fullness of God's presence. Also symbolized are the seven Asian churches that receive the letters of chs. [[Revelation 2#v1|2]]; [[Revelation 3#v1|3]]. This reference prompts some scholars to suggest John was in the church in a liturgical setting during the Revelation.

> [!info] 1:11a ^fvar781
> NU-Text and M-Text omit *I am *through third *and. *

> [!info] 1:11b ^fvar782
> NU-Text and M-Text omit* which are in Asia.*

### [[Revelation 1#v13|1:13]]
^v13
> [!note] 1:13 ^f6062
> **One like the Son of Man** recalls Daniel's messianic figure ([[Daniel 7#v13|Dan 7:13]]—repeated by Stephen at his martyrdom, [[Acts 7#v56|Acts 7:56]]). Christ called Himself *Son of Man* (see especially [[Matthew 24#v30|Mt 24:30ff]].), for He is the fulfillment of Daniel's prophecy. Additional parallels may be seen in both the Old and New Testaments ([[Daniel 10#v6|Dan 10:6]]; [[Matthew 17#v2|Mt 17:2]]; [[Ephesians 6#v17|Eph 6:17]]; [[Hebrews 4#v12|Heb 4:12]]). We also see Him vested in high-priestly garments (see [[Exodus 28#v4|Ex 28:4]]; [[Exodus 29#v5|29:5]]; [[Leviticus 16#v4|Lv 16:4]]; WSol [[Wisdom of Solomon 18#v24|18:24]]; [[Zechariah 3#v4|Zec 3:4]], [[Zechariah 3#v5|5]]). The gold with which He is girded is both royal ([[I Maccabees 10#v89|1Mc 10:89]]) and priestly.

### [[Revelation 1#v14|1:14-16]]
^v14
> [!note] 1:14-16 ^f6063
> Further, Christ is here described as God, His **hair** (v. [[Revelation 1#v14|14]]) being that of Daniel's vision of God as the “Ancient of Days” ([[Revelation 7#v9|7:9]]; see also 1En 46:1). His **eyes** signify knowledge; His **feet** (v. [[Revelation 1#v15|15]]), permanence and stability; His **voice**, authority or teaching; His **right hand** (v. [[Revelation 1#v16|16]]), power; His **two-edged sword**, complete discernment. This imagery continues throughout Revelation to affirm the preexistence and eternal divinity of the Son of Man (see also [[John 1#v1|Jn 1:1–18]]). Thus, in Christ man (v. [[Revelation 1#v14|14]]) and God (vv. [[Revelation 1#v15|15]], [[Revelation 1#v16|16]]) are united.

### [[Revelation 1#v15|1:15]]
^v15
> [!note] 1:15 ^f6064
> [[Daniel 2#v31|Dan 2:31–44]] indicates this mysterious metal foundation not only provides stability but has the ability to forcibly crush all opposition as well. These images are contrasted with the feet of clay found in [[Daniel 2#v33|Dan 2:33]], [[Daniel 2#v43|43]]: the kingdoms of this world are not permanent, nor ultimately triumphant.

### [[Revelation 1#v16|1:16]]
^v16
> [!note] 1:16 ^f6065
> The Lord holds the **stars** that represent the seven churches ([[Revelation 1#v20|1:20]]), and hence, the Church. For Christ is Lord of the Church. In His just judgment, the sword He wields is the Word of God, which cuts effortlessly to the very marrow and heart of humanity (see [[Revelation 2#v16|2:16]]; [[Revelation 18#v15|18:15]]; [[Isaiah 11#v4|Is 11:4]]; [[Isaiah 49#v2|49:2]]; WSol [[Wisdom of Solomon 18#v15|18:15]]; [[Ephesians 6#v17|Eph 6:17]]; [[II Thessalonians 2#v9|2Th 2:9]]; [[Hebrews 4#v12|Heb 4:12]]). The brilliance of His face recalls the Uncreated Light John saw radiating from the Savior at the Transfiguration on Mount Tabor.

### [[Revelation 1#v17|1:17-18]]
^v17
> [!note] 1:17-18 ^f6066
> Mortal humanity cannot bear the revelation of divine glory, a frequent biblical theme (see [[Exodus 19#v21|Ex 19:21]]; [[Exodus 33#v20|33:20]]; [[Isaiah 6#v5|Is 6:5]]). Just as he fell prostrate at Mount Tabor ([[Matthew 17#v6|Mt 17:6]]), so also does John here, in the presence of the glorified Savior (see [[Ezekiel 1#v27|Ezk 1:27]]; [[Daniel 10#v7|Dan 10:7–9]]; see also 1En 14:24). **Do not be afraid** is a revelatory formula from the OT. As a signal for the theophany, it was carried over into the NT (see the Annunciation, [[Luke 1#v30|Lk 1:30]]; Jesus walking upon the water, [[Matthew 14#v27|Mt 14:27]]—“Fear not, I AM”; the Transfiguration, [[Matthew 17#v7|Mt 17:7]]). In the OT God was called “the first and the last” ([[Isaiah 44#v6|Is 44:6]]; [[Isaiah 48#v12|48:12]]), and so, too, is the Messiah. Some early heresies (e.g. Docetism) held that Jesus only seemed to die. But the Lord Himself testified, **I **. . .** was dead**, affirming the authenticity of His death; **alive forevermore**, His Resurrection—the power of which effects His lordship over death and its realm. The Orthodox icon of the resurrected Christ depicts Him with these **keys** (v. [[Revelation 1#v18|18]]) in hand, standing triumphantly on the open gates of **Hades**.

> [!info] 1:17a ^fvar783
> NU-Text and M-Text omit *to me.*

### [[Revelation 1#v20|1:20]]
^v20
> [!note] 1:20 ^f6068
> **The angels of the seven churches** have been variously interpreted as being (1) the guardian angels of the church communities; (2) the pastoral leadership of these local churches; (3) a personification of the prevailing spirit of the given congregations; or (4) simply the messengers responsible for delivering the letters. The term “angel” (heavenly or earthly messenger) is used over 60 times in Revelation.

> [!info] 1:20a ^fvar785
> NU-Text and M-Text omit *which you saw.*
