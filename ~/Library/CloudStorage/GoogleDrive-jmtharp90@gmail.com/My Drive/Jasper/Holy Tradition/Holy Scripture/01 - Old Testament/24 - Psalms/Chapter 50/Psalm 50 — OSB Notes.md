### [[Psalm 50#v1|50:1]]
^v1
> [!note] 50:1 ^f2046
> [[Psalms 50#v1|Ps 50]] teaches the nature of genuine repentance, which is the reason it is used so often in the services of the Church. Historically, King David committed the sins and transgressions of adultery and murder (vv. [[Psalms 50#v1|1]], [[Psalms 50#v2|2]]; see also [[II Kingdoms 11#v1|2Kg 11]]; [[II Kingdoms 12#v1|12]]). However, He obtained mercy from God because of his repentance. Yet many fall into sins but never come to repentance; therefore, no one can use David's sins as an excuse to sin. For sins and transgressions in themselves do not draw down the mercy of God. Genuine repentance, however, does attract His mercy, for He sees it and responds accordingly (v. [[Psalms 50#v3|3]]). So genuine repentance is characterized by such things as confession to God (vv. [[Psalms 50#v4|4–8]]); inward purity (vv. [[Psalms 50#v12|12–16]]); and inward humility (vv. [[Psalms 50#v17|17–21]]).

> [!liturgy] 50:1 ^fx95
> [[Psalms 50#v1|Ps 50]] is used daily in the services of Orthros, the Third Hour, and Compline, and it is also recited in every Divine Liturgy by the priest as he censes before the Great Entrance.
